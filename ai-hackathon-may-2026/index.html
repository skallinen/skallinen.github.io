The Shift

From Source Code
To Source Docs

We have started thinking how the emphasis is shifting, and we have been developing techniques for this.

The Problem

We had 0 domain knowledge when we started.

What We Did

Phase 1: Gathering Context

  • Discussed and workshopped with the team.
  • Discussed with Eli.
  • Transcribed a video interview with a domain expert that Eli had prepared before the hackathon.
What We Did

Phase 2: LLM Deep Research

  • Fed the interview to an LLM to generate a general deep research prompt.
  • Explained the domain in the Nordics: what it is, what tech is used, what are the actors.
  • This research utilized around 50 sources (web pages, PDFs, etc).
What We Did

Phase 3: The Wiki Structure

  • Used our Wiki skill to create a compiled Wiki structure.
  • Fed the 50 external articles into the Wiki.
  • The LLM compiled them directly into the Wiki format.
The Results

Testing the Output

  • First test: Explain why Gemini Terrain exists.
  • The results were very good. It explained it well with much more relevant detail than we had managed to collect in our preparative discussions.
  • Generated a Product Requirement Document (PRD) including an implementation task list.
  • (Then we got stuck with trying to get certs working with .NET in our local environment...)
Takeaways

Team Golf: 3 Core Learnings

  • AI Eliminates Bottlenecks By automating both code generation and the maintenance of documentation, AI eliminates traditional development bottlenecks and enables immediate project execution regardless of prior domain expertise.
  • Dependency Injection for Knowledge Eli’s interview with the expert was essentially an essential dependency injection for your team's domain knowledge base. As it was the source for much of the understanding. This is worth focusing on.
  • The Natural Collaboration Space The wiki is the natural space for dev, business, and product collaboration, where they can naturally meet.
