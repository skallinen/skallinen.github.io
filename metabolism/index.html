Architecture is Metabolism

How AI Changes the Rules. And Which Ones Still Matter.

Created by

Niklas Höglund  ·  Per Nissiä  ·  Sami Kallinen

"To get the benefits of new technology, we must also change the rules that were designed to accommodate the limitation that the technology has now diminished."

— Eliyahu M. Goldratt

Hook

No deploys on Fridays.

When was that rule written?

What was it protecting?

Is that thing still real?

Every rule presents itself as protection. The work is figuring out what it is actually protecting.

Rules are the outermost layer. An organisation has a goal. Qualities help it reach that goal. Rules exist to cultivate those qualities. The rule is the surface. The quality is what matters.

Some rules are easy to audit: the limitation is gone, the rule is wrong, update it. Some require expertise to unpick. Some only reveal themselves through probing. The answer is not visible until something changes.

Reframe

An organisation is people united around a shared goal.

Qualities become important to that goal. Some are visible. Some are invisible until they're gone.

Cultivates quality
  • No deploys on Fridays (protects stability)
  • Every change needs a reviewer (protects understanding)
Manages a limitation
  • Document only critical paths (writing more was too expensive)
  • Deploy once a quarter (deploys used to be risky)

The question, for any rule, is whether it is still cultivating the quality it was built for.

Reframe

Architecture is not the diagram.

Architecture is the set of qualities your system actually has.

How understandable it is. How safely you can change it. How quickly it recovers.

The diagram on the wiki is at best a description. More qualities means better architecture.

Qualities can be measured. Not always directly, but proxied.

How often do you deploy without incident?

How many people can safely change a given module?

How long does recovery take when something breaks?

Do engineers sweat before they push?

Architecture decisions are choices: which qualities to cultivate and which trade-offs to accept. The diagram describes what resulted. It does not produce it.

Reframe

A product is not what was shipped.

It is the compounding output of people, skills, and interactions. Sustained over time.

P(t)  =  (people  ×  skills  ×  interactions)t

Each factor compounds the others over time. What was shipped is a snapshot. What the organisation is capable of is the accumulation.*

The interesting question isn't what did we ship?
It's what is this organisation currently capable of sustaining?

* A more accurate representation is recursive: P(0) = initial capability;  P(t) = P(t−1) × (people  ·  skills  ·  interactions). Each result becomes the base for the next period, the way compound interest works. Harder to read; closer to what actually happens.

The Metaphor

Fred Brooks, 1986:

"The secret is that it is grown, not built."

He was right that the building metaphor fails.

But the organism metaphor alone doesn't go far enough.

An organism tells you what something is.

Metabolism tells you what something does to stay alive.

The Metaphor

Metabolism has four useful properties.

Property What it means for software
Adaptive Always self-adjusting. Not a fixed structure: every decision, every incident, every release shapes it. The architecture you have is the result of everything that happened to it.
Distributed No central controller. Every cell does its part locally. The real architecture lives in what people actually do, not in what any diagram says they should.
Continuous Metabolism does not pause. A codebase not being actively maintained is not in stasis. It is breaking down. There is no neutral state.
Produces waste A working metabolism generates byproducts. Technical debt, legacy patterns, accumulated workarounds: normal outputs of a functioning system. When waste stops being managed, the organism gets sick.
The Metaphor
"A codebase that is not being actively maintained is not stable.
It is degrading."

There is no neutral state.

Choosing not to invest in your system is a decision. It just looks like inaction.

Qualities are Emergent

You cannot
design quality.

Understandability, resilience, recoverability. None of these arrive by decision.

They emerge from what teams actually do every day: the habits, the pressures, the constraints. Compounded over time.

"If a team is afraid to deploy on Fridays, the system is fragile. Regardless of what the documentation says."

Qualities are Emergent

Practices are the levers of emergence.

No practice produces its quality directly. They produce the conditions from which quality emerges.

Write the press release first
→
Forces clarity on user value before a line of code is written. Collaborative formulation of the why.
Rotate devs through customer support
→
You handle support for the code you wrote. When a user is confused or something breaks, you have to diagnose it fast. That closes the feedback loop between writing code and living with the consequences of it.
Test-driven development
→
Forces modularity before implementation. Code that is hard to test is almost always too tightly coupled.

Resistance is diagnostic. When a team resists a practice, that's not a bad attitude. It's a symptom of the system's current health. The practice is the probe. What it is telling you depends on what kind of situation you are in.

Case Study
"AI does not fix a big ball of mud."

It makes the mud faster to produce.

When AI generates the artifact of a phase, which practices ran?

If the practices did not run, which rules were enforced?

If those rules were not enforced, which qualities emerged?

The answer is not always obvious. Some gaps are visible immediately. Some require analysis. Some only become clear in retrospect.

How to Respond

Not every part of this is the same kind of problem.

Dropping a rule, keeping it, or probing it: each requires knowing what kind of situation you are in.

Knowing which rules to update is the easier problem. Getting the new practices to actually run, so the new qualities can emerge, is harder. That is metabolism.

Some of what AI has changed is clear: the old rule is just wrong, no experiment needed.

Some is complicated: analysis and expertise can get you to a good answer.

Some is complex: cause and effect only visible in retrospect. The right move is to probe.

And some is chaotic. When a practice has collapsed faster than anyone noticed, the ground is still moving. Treating chaos as complexity is how organisations make it worse.

How to Respond

Different states require different responses.

Clear
Sense → Categorise → Respond
Cause and effect are obvious. Best practice exists. Apply it directly.
If documentation is no longer expensive to produce, the rule that said "document only the essentials" is just wrong. Update it.
Complicated
Sense → Analyse → Respond
Multiple good answers. Analysis and expertise can get you there. Good practice, not best practice.
Where to introduce AI agents in your pipeline is knowable, but requires judgment and domain expertise.
Complex
Probe → Sense → Respond
Cause and effect only visible in retrospect. No right answer in advance. Run small experiments. Watch what changes. Adjust.
Which skills atrophy when agents take over? Which gaps grow faster and stay invisible longest? You won't know until you look.
Chaotic
Act → Stabilise → Sense
Accountability has eroded. A practice has collapsed. Don't analyse. Act to stabilise first, then sense, then respond.
Assign a historian: someone capturing what is actually happening and why, in real time, so there's something to learn from when the chaos settles.

Takeaways

Five questions to take back.

  1. What qualities does your organisation actually care about? Not what the architecture says. What do your team's daily habits actually produce?
  2. Which of your rules are managing limitations that no longer exist? Rules that cultivate qualities: keep. Rules that managed costs AI has changed: question.
  3. What is your team's resistance telling you? Resistance is diagnostic, not disciplinary. It tells you which qualities the system currently lacks the health to support.
  4. Where has the limitation moved? AI helped with code generation. If that was the limitation, it has moved. Is it now in understanding what to build? In shared knowledge of why decisions were made?
  5. Is your metabolism healthy? Are the daily practices that produce your qualities actually running? A process that quietly stops does not announce itself. The qualities just stop emerging.
"Metabolism does not finish.
It either continues or it stops."

The AI transition is not a problem an organisation solves and moves on from. It is a new set of conditions the organism now has to metabolise, continuously, the way it always has.
